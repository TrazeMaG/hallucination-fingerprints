% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
When a language model confidently states that the capital of
Germany is Paris, something has gone wrong inside the model
before that word ever appears. This paper investigates what.
We ask whether hallucinations follow \emph{consistent, detectable
patterns} in the internal activations of transformer models
prior to the generation of an incorrect token, and find
that they do. Through experiments on a custom 806K-parameter
transformer and GPT-2 (124M parameters), tested across 20,000
factual prompts in 7 knowledge categories, we identify two named
phenomena. \textbf{Relation Dropout}: attention to the semantic
relation token collapses in the final transformer block of small
models before a hallucination occurs.
\textbf{Last-Layer Suppression}: factual knowledge emerges
correctly in blocks 10--11 of GPT-2 but is systematically
overridden by block 12.
We propose a three-type hallucination taxonomy, release
\textbf{HallScan} (\texttt{pip install hallscan}), an
open-source detection tool, and \textbf{HallBench}, a labeled
benchmark of 20,000 annotated examples.
\end{abstract}

% ── 1. Introduction ───────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}

Consider the following exchange with GPT-2:

\begin{center}
\textit{``The capital of Germany is''} $\rightarrow$
\textbf{``the''} \quad (confidence: 89.1\%)
\end{center}

The model is wrong. But more striking than the error is the
confidence. GPT-2 does not hedge. It does not say ``I think.''
It predicts ``the'' as though Berlin were never a possibility.

Yet Berlin \emph{was} a possibility inside the model.
Our layer-by-layer analysis reveals that at block 11,
the probability of ``Berlin'' reaches 34.7\%. The model
has found the answer. Then block 12 erases it, and ``the''
wins with 41.2\% probability. The model knew. Then it forgot.

This paper is about that forgetting. Specifically, we ask:
\emph{do language models hallucinate in consistent, detectable
patterns visible in their internal activations before the
incorrect token is generated?}

We show that such patterns exist. They form the basis of a
new class of hallucination detectors that intercept errors
during generation rather than catching them post-hoc.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make the following contributions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify and name \textbf{Relation Dropout}: in
    small transformer models, attention to the semantic relation
    token (e.g., ``capital'') collapses in the final block
    immediately before a hallucination. This occurs even when
    the entity token (e.g., ``germany'') is strongly attended to.

    \item We identify and name \textbf{Last-Layer Suppression}:
    in GPT-2, factual knowledge emerges with high probability
    in blocks 10--11, then block 12 systematically suppresses
    this signal in favour of high-frequency structural tokens.
    This pattern holds across all 20,000 tested prompts,
    with an average suppression layer of exactly 12.0.

    \item We propose a \textbf{three-type hallucination taxonomy}
    (Type~1: Relation Dropout; Type~2a: Last-Layer Suppression;
    Type~2b: Knowledge Gap) grounded in internal activation
    analysis rather than output-level evaluation.

    \item We discover \textbf{prompt sensitivity} as a
    measurable phenomenon: the same factual query phrased
    differently produces 8.1\% accuracy in one form and 0.0\%
    in another, suggesting GPT-2 has learned surface-form
    patterns rather than abstract facts.

    \item We validate our findings at scale across
    \textbf{20,000 factual prompts} in 7 knowledge categories
    on an NVIDIA RTX 4060 GPU.

    \item We release \textbf{HallScan}
    (\texttt{pip install hallscan}), an open-source Python
    library for hallucination detection, and \textbf{HallBench},
    a labeled benchmark of 20,000 annotated examples on
    HuggingFace.
\end{itemize}

% ── 2. Related Work ───────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Hallucination in NLG.}
\citet{ji2023survey} provide a comprehensive taxonomy of
hallucination in natural language generation, distinguishing
intrinsic errors (contradicting source material) from extrinsic
ones (unverifiable claims). \citet{lin2022truthfulqa} introduce
TruthfulQA, demonstrating that larger models are not necessarily
more truthful. This counterintuitive finding motivates our
investigation of internal mechanisms rather than scale.

\paragraph{Factuality and calibration.}
\citet{kadavath2022language} study model self-knowledge,
finding that well-calibrated models can identify their own
uncertainty. Our work complements this by showing that even
when a model ``knows'' the answer (as evidenced by high
probability in intermediate layers), output-level calibration
may still fail due to Last-Layer Suppression.

\paragraph{Mechanistic interpretability.}
\citet{elhage2021mathematical} establish a mathematical
framework for transformer circuits. \citet{olsson2022context}
identify induction heads as key in-context learning mechanisms.
We extend this interpretability tradition to the specific problem
of factual hallucination, examining which components fail and
at which layer.

\paragraph{Knowledge in FFN layers.}
\citet{geva2021transformer} demonstrate that feed-forward
layers function as key-value memories. Our finding that factual
knowledge emerges in blocks 10--11 before being overridden by
block 12 is consistent with this view: the FFN layers may
correctly retrieve factual associations, which are then
suppressed by a final output-selection mechanism.

% ── 3. Method ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Method}

\subsection{Models}

\paragraph{Custom transformer (806K parameters).}
We train a GPT-style decoder-only transformer with 4 blocks,
4 attention heads, and $d_{\text{model}} = 128$ on a curated
corpus of factual capital-city and geographic statements
(e.g., ``the capital of france is paris''), repeated to produce
8,350 training tokens. We train for 10 epochs using the Adam
optimiser \citep{kingma2014adam} with learning rate
$3 \times 10^{-4}$ and observe loss converging to near zero,
demonstrating overfitting to the training distribution.
This controlled environment allows us to study hallucination
in a setting where training data is fully known.

\paragraph{GPT-2 (124M parameters).}
We use the standard GPT-2 model \citep{radford2019language}
pretrained on 40GB of internet text (WebText). This model
serves as our production-scale validation environment,
representative of the class of autoregressive language models
deployed in practice.

\subsection{Internal Activation Capture}

For each prompt, we record:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Attention weights} in every block averaged
    across all heads, with particular focus on attention
    directed to the relation token.

    \item \textbf{Hidden state projections} at each block,
    obtained by passing the residual stream through the
    model's final layer norm and unembedding matrix. This
    allows us to compute the implied vocabulary distribution
    at each intermediate layer, revealing what the model
    ``would predict'' if generation stopped at that block.

    \item \textbf{Top-10 predictions} and their probabilities
    at the final output layer.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hallucination Taxonomy}
\label{sec:taxonomy}

We classify each incorrect prediction into one of three types:

\paragraph{Type~1: Relation Dropout.}
Average attention to the relation token in the final block
falls below $\tau = 0.05$. The model fails to route attention
to the semantically critical word that constrains the answer
type. This type is characteristic of small, undertrained models.

\paragraph{Type~2a: Last-Layer Suppression.}
The correct answer appears in the top-10 predictions at the
final output layer but is not the top-1 prediction. Internal
layer analysis reveals the correct answer peaked in blocks
10--11 before being suppressed by block 12.

\paragraph{Type~2b: Knowledge Gap.}
The correct answer does not appear in the top-10 predictions.
The relevant fact was not encoded during pretraining.

% ── 4. Experiments ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Experiment 1: Relation Dropout in Small Models}

We test our 806K-parameter model on 6 capital-city prompts
of the form ``The capital of [country] is'' and measure
average attention to the relation token ``capital'' in the
final transformer block across all heads. Results are shown
in Table~\ref{tab:relation_dropout} and visualised in
Figure~\ref{fig:relation_dropout}.

\subsection{Experiment 2: GPT-2 Validation}

We test GPT-2 on 35 factual prompts across 5 categories
(capitals, inventors, authors, science, history) and classify
each prediction using our taxonomy from
Section~\ref{sec:taxonomy}. Results are shown in
Table~\ref{tab:gpt2}.

\subsection{Experiment 3: Layer-by-Layer Suppression Analysis}

For 4 prompts exhibiting Type~2a behaviour, we project the
hidden state at each of GPT-2's 12 transformer blocks to
vocabulary space, producing per-layer probability curves for
both the correct answer and the competing token. Results are
shown in Figure~\ref{fig:layer_suppression} and
Table~\ref{tab:layers}.

\subsection{Experiment 4: Large-Scale GPU Validation}

Using an NVIDIA RTX 4060 Laptop GPU, we run 20,000 factual
prompts through GPT-2 at 47 prompts per second (7.1 minutes
total) across 7 knowledge categories. Prompts are generated
from 50 country-capital pairs with 3 phrasing variants each,
15 scientist-discovery pairs, 10 historical dates, 10 science
facts, and 10 author-work pairs. Results are summarised in
Table~\ref{tab:largescale} and Figure~\ref{fig:taxonomy}.

\subsection{Last-Layer Suppression (Experiment 3)}

Figure~\ref{fig:layer_suppression} reveals the most striking
finding of this paper. For every hallucination prompt tested,
the probability of the correct answer follows the same
trajectory: near-zero through blocks 1--9, a sharp surge in
blocks 10--11, then a cliff-edge collapse at block 12 as the
structural token ``the'' takes over.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figure1_layer_suppression.png}
\caption{Per-layer probability of the correct answer versus
the winning incorrect token across GPT-2's 12 blocks.
The green shaded region (blocks 10--11) marks factual
emergence; the red region (block 12) marks suppression.
The Berlin Wall case (bottom right) survives because its
factual signal exceeds the suppressor token probability
at block 12, illustrating the survival threshold.}
\label{fig:layer_suppression}
\end{figure}

The Berlin Wall case is instructive. Block 12 suppresses
the ``1989'' signal from 0.128 to 0.0002, a 640-fold
reduction. Yet ``1989'' remains the top prediction because
``the'' only reaches 0.045. This defines a
\emph{survival threshold}: a factual signal strong enough
to resist block 12 interference. Facts appearing frequently
in training survive; rare facts do not.

The scale validation confirms both named phenomena.
The average suppression layer across 20,000 prompts is
\textbf{exactly 12.0}: block 12 suppresses in every
measurable case. The average peak factual layer is
\textbf{11.1}, consistent with our targeted analysis.

The prompt sensitivity finding warrants particular attention.
The prompt ``The capital of France is'' achieves 8.1\%
accuracy on capital queries. The prompt ``France's capital
city is'' achieves 0.0\% on identical facts. The model has
not learned that Paris is the capital of France in any
abstract sense. It has learned that the token sequence
``The capital of [country] is'' sometimes precedes a city
name. This surface-form dependency is a fundamental
limitation of next-token prediction as a mechanism for
factual storage.

% ── 6. Discussion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}

\paragraph{Two failure modes, one confidence.}
Relation Dropout and Last-Layer Suppression represent
fundamentally different failure mechanisms, yet both produce
the same observable output: a confident wrong answer.
This has important implications for output-level hallucination
detection. Confidence scores alone cannot distinguish between
a model that never found the fact (Type~1) and a model that
found it then lost it (Type~2a). Internal activation analysis
is necessary to distinguish these cases.

\paragraph{The survival threshold.}
Our results suggest a frequency-based survival threshold:
facts that appear with sufficient frequency in training data
generate factual signals strong enough to survive block 12
suppression. ``The Berlin Wall fell in 1989'' and ``Water
is made of hydrogen and oxygen'' both survive.
``The capital of Australia is Canberra'' does not. This
implies that hallucination rate is partially predictable
from training data statistics, independent of model
architecture.

\paragraph{Block 12 as a structural bottleneck.}
The consistency of block 12 as the suppression layer across
all tested prompts suggests it may function as a structural
pattern completion layer, one that has learned the syntactic
form of the output regardless of factual content.
Targeted interventions at block 12, such as activation
patching or fine-tuning with factual emphasis, may offer
a direct path to reducing Last-Layer Suppression.

\paragraph{Implications for HallScan.}
HallScan operationalises our taxonomy as a practical
detection tool. By measuring relation attention in the final
block and tracking the peak factual layer, HallScan
classifies hallucination risk without requiring access to
the correct answer. This is a key advantage for deployment
in settings where ground truth is unavailable.

% ── 7. Limitations ────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations}

Our study has several limitations that future work should
address.

First, the Relation Dropout threshold $\tau = 0.05$ was
determined empirically on a small number of prompts and may
not generalise across architectures or knowledge domains.
A learned threshold trained on a larger annotated set would
be more robust.

Second, our large-scale experiment generates 20,000 prompts
from 210 base templates. While this tests the consistency
of our findings, it does not provide 20,000 independent
factual queries. Future work should construct a larger
independent prompt set spanning diverse knowledge domains.

Third, we test only GPT-2 at production scale. Whether
Last-Layer Suppression occurs in larger models (GPT-3,
LLaMA, Claude) and at which layer remains an open question.
The phenomenon may manifest at different depths in models
with more blocks.

Fourth, our taxonomy is based on observable output behaviour
and attention patterns. Deeper causal analysis using
activation patching \citep{elhage2021mathematical} would
allow us to establish whether block 12 suppression is
causally responsible for Type~2a hallucinations, rather
than merely correlated with them.

% ── 8. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}

Language models do not hallucinate randomly. We have shown
that two consistent, measurable patterns precede hallucinations
in the internal activations of transformer models: Relation
Dropout in small models, and Last-Layer Suppression in GPT-2.
Both patterns are detectable before the incorrect token is
generated, suggesting that hallucination is not an inevitable
property of next-token prediction, but a specific failure
mode with identifiable internal signatures.

The most striking result of this paper is the consistency
of block 12 suppression. Across 20,000 prompts, the average
suppression layer is exactly 12.0. The model finds the
answer. Then one layer erases it. Understanding why, and
intervening, is the work this paper sets in motion.
